    02 — The companion app

    The phone does the typing. The bike does the riding.

    A free iOS app handles everything that’s fiddly on a touch e-paper screen — planning routes, building offline maps, and reviewing rides — then streams it all to the head unit over Bluetooth.

    03 — What runs where

    Everything you need on the bike. The phone just helps you set up.

    Once it’s loaded, the head unit rides completely on its own — no phone, no signal, no account. You only reach for the app now and then: to plan a new route, add maps for a new area, or change a setting.

    On the device — every ride
    • GPS tracking with speed, distance and a live map
    • Records each ride to a .fit file on the SD card
    • Turn-by-turn prompts along a loaded route
    • Pairs with heart-rate & power sensors over Bluetooth
    • Runs fully offline — no phone or signal needed
    In the app — now and then
    • Plan a route and send it to the device
    • Build offline maps for a new area
    • Pull rides off the device and review them
    • Change settings and update the firmware

    04 — Supported hardware

    One board today.

    OpenTrailPaper runs on off-the-shelf hardware you buy and flash yourself. Today that means a single board — here’s honestly what it’s good at and where it falls short.

    LilyGO T5S3 4.7" E-Paper PRO↗

    ESP32-S3 · 16 MB flash · 8 MB PSRAM · 960×540 e-paper · BLE 5

    Supported

    Strengths

    • A 4.7" e-paper panel that stays readable in direct sun and sips power
    • Enough flash and PSRAM to hold offline map tiles for a whole region
    • Capacitive touch, an SD slot and USB-C, so no extra parts to solder
    • Bluetooth 5 for heart-rate, power and cadence sensors
    • Cheap, easy to buy, and flashable straight from your browser

    Tradeoffs

    • No pressure sensor. There’s no barometric altimeter, so climbing is derived from elevation baked into the map tiles rather than measured — total ascent is an estimate.
    • Basic GPS. The receiver works, but a modern multi-band module would lock on faster and hold a fix better under tree cover or between buildings.
    • No magnetometer. Without a compass the map can only orient from your direction of travel, so it can’t point the right way while you’re stopped.
    • Modest battery. Fine for a long day out, but a bigger cell would carry you through multi-day trips without a recharge.
    • Weak hardware buttons. Most interaction goes through the touch panel, which is fiddly with winter gloves or in the rain.
    • No waterproofing. The board ships bare with no seal or IP rating, so it needs a case or a bag of its own before you ride it in the wet.

    More boards

    Support for larger hardware is coming

    Coming soon

    Work is under way to run OpenTrailPaper on bigger, better-equipped hardware — the kind that closes the gaps above with a barometric altimeter for real ascent, a multi-band GPS, a magnetometer for a true compass, a larger battery and proper tactile buttons you can hit with gloves on.
